How to Give Feedback to Your Manager (and Make It Stick)

Giving feedback to someone more senior than you lands when you set it up correctly with permission and framing, then deliver it with specific, observable facts and a clear impact.

Most people avoid giving upward feedback. They fear retaliation, awkwardness, or that it just won't make a difference. These are valid fears if you go about it wrong. But your manager needs your perspective. They have blind spots. Good managers want to improve. Your job is to make it easy and safe for them to hear it.

## Forget Anonymous Feedback

Anonymous feedback feels safe. It is not. It poisons trust. Your manager knows the feedback is about them, but doesn't know *who* it's from. They can't ask clarifying questions. They can't improve effectively. They might assume the worst. It also makes you look unwilling to own your perspective. If you want change, you need to own the conversation. You need to be direct.

## The Permission-and-Framing Setup

This is your first critical step. Do not skip it. This setup signals respect, intent, and professionalism. It makes your manager receptive.

### Step 1: Get Permission

Don't ambush your manager. Schedule time. State your intent upfront. This gives them time to prepare mentally. It also shows you respect their time and position.

**Here’s what you say:**

> 

"Hey [Manager's Name], do you have 15 minutes later today or tomorrow? I have some feedback I'd like to share regarding [specific area, e.g., 'how we run our team meetings'] and I want to make sure I deliver it well."

Or:

> 

"Could I block 15 minutes on your calendar for Thursday? I have a perspective on [Project X] I think could be helpful."

This isn't a vague request. It's specific enough to manage expectations. You're not saying "I have a problem with you." You're saying "I have information that could help."

### Step 2: Frame Your Intent

When you sit down for the conversation, start by clearly stating your positive intent. This disarms potential defensiveness. It shows you're on their side, working towards a shared goal.

**Here’s what you say:**

> 

"My goal here isn't to criticize. It's to help us [achieve shared goal, e.g., 'make our team meetings more productive so we can hit our Q3 targets'] / [make your job easier] / [improve team morale]."

Or:

> 

"I'm bringing this up because I care about the success of [Project Y] and I see an opportunity for us to improve."

Connect it to something that matters to them: team performance, project success, their own goals, company objectives. You are not complaining. You are identifying a problem and offering a solution for a shared benefit.

## The Structure for Recurring Behavior

Once you have permission and a clear frame, use this structure. It focuses on observable facts, not judgment. This is crucial for feedback to land without triggering defensiveness.

- 

**Situation:** When did this happen? Be precise. Focus on specific events, not generalities.

- 

**Behavior:** What did they do? Stick to facts. What did you see or hear? Avoid interpretation or assumptions about their intent.

- 

**Impact:** What was the result? Focus on objective consequences on you, the team, the project, or the company goal. How did it affect productivity, morale, deadlines, or outcomes?

- 

**Request:** What do you need? Be clear, actionable, and solution-oriented. What specific change would you like to see?

### Scenario: Your Manager Consistently Interrupts

You've noticed your manager frequently cuts people off in team meetings, especially you.

**Permission & Framing:**

> 

"Hey [Manager's Name], I wanted to chat about our team syncs. My goal is to make sure everyone feels heard and that we get through all critical updates efficiently."

**Feedback Delivery:**

> 

"**Situation:** In our last three team syncs on Tuesday mornings,

**Behavior:** you've interrupted me or others when we were presenting project updates before we finished our thoughts.

**Impact:** This makes me feel unheard, and I've noticed others start to disengage or struggle to get their full point across. It also means we sometimes miss key information or go over time because we have to repeat ourselves.

**Request:** What I need is for us to let each person finish their update before moving on. Could we try holding questions until the end of each person's slot, or maybe use a 'parking lot' for longer discussions?"

### Scenario: Your Manager Micromanages Your Tasks

Your manager asks for daily detailed updates on tasks you've already marked as complete or in progress in your project management tool.

**Permission & Framing:**

> 

"[Manager's Name], I'd like to offer some feedback on our project check-ins. My goal is to streamline our workflow so we can hit our aggressive Q3 deadlines more effectively."

**Feedback Delivery:**

> 

"**Situation:** On the [Project X] last week, and again on [Project Y] this week,

**Behavior:** you asked for daily detailed updates on tasks that are already green in Asana and have been assigned to me.

**Impact:** This takes me about an hour each day to compile, which pulls me away from the actual work. It also signals a lack of trust, which demotivates me and the team, and slows down our progress towards the deadline.

**Request:** I'm confident in managing my tasks independently. Would you be open to trusting the Asana updates, and I'll proactively flag if I hit a blocker? We can stick to our weekly sync for deeper dives."

## Handling Pushback in the Moment

Your manager might get defensive. It's a natural human reaction. Don't back down, but don't fight. Listen. Acknowledge. Reiterate.

**1. Listen Actively:** Let them speak. Don't interrupt. Understand their perspective.

**2. Acknowledge Their Perspective:** Show you've heard them, even if you don't agree.

> 

"I understand you're trying to stay on top of things / ensure quality / manage project risk."

**3. Reiterate Your Framing and Impact:** Gently bring it back to the shared goal and the objective consequences.

> 

"My intention isn't to add to your workload. It's to ensure we're all as efficient as possible so we can achieve [shared goal]. The impact I'm seeing is [reiterate specific impact]. How can we address that together?"

**4. Focus on Collaboration:** Shift from "my problem with you" to "our problem to solve."

> 

"Are you open to trying [proposed solution] for a week or two and seeing if it improves things?"

"What are your thoughts on how we could adjust this?"

If they dismiss it entirely, don't press. You've planted the seed. They might reflect later. You've also shown you're willing to have difficult conversations professionally. That builds your reputation.

## Why This Approach Works

This isn't about blaming. It's about problem-solving. You're showing respect by asking for permission. You're demonstrating a shared goal with your framing. You're being objective with your facts. You're offering a solution. This approach positions you as a valuable contributor, not a complainer. It builds trust and shows leadership, regardless of your title.
