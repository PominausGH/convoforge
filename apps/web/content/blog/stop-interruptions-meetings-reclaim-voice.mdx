What to Say When Someone Interrupts You in a Meeting

When someone interrupts you in a meeting, the most effective responses are direct, calm, and focused on regaining the floor, not on shaming the interrupter. You need to re-establish your right to speak and finish your thought without derailing the meeting or appearing defensive.

You’re mid-sentence. You’re making a critical point. Then, someone jumps in. Your manager, a colleague, even a direct report. Your blood pressure rises. Your train of thought derails. You feel dismissed. It’s frustrating, unprofessional, and happens constantly. Ignoring it means your ideas get lost. Reacting poorly means you look unprofessional.

This isn't just about politeness. It's about influence, visibility, and getting your message heard. Your career depends on it.

## The Two Responses That Backfire

Most people react to interruptions in one of two ways. Both are understandable. Both are ineffective. Avoid them.

### 1. The Silent Retreat

You stop talking. You let the interrupter take over. Maybe you're trying to be polite. Maybe you're caught off guard. Maybe you hope they'll pause and invite you back in. They won't. Or they rarely will. This teaches others that your voice is optional. Your ideas are secondary. You lose the floor and often, the opportunity to make your point.

**Scenario:** You're explaining a project timeline. A colleague jumps in with a tangent about a different project.

**What you do:** You trail off. You nod. You hope to jump back in later.

**Why it backfires:** You concede your turn. Your point is lost. The meeting moves on. Your silence is interpreted as agreement or lack of conviction.

### 2. The Aggressive Confrontation

You snap. "Excuse me, I wasn't finished!" or "Can you let me speak?" This might feel good in the moment. It rarely achieves the desired outcome. You sound defensive. You might embarrass the interrupter, creating tension. The focus shifts from your ideas to your reaction. People remember your tone, not your point.

**Scenario:** You're presenting data. A senior leader cuts you off with a speculative question that undermines your finding.

**What you do:** "I really wish you'd let me finish my sentence!"

**Why it backfires:** You come across as petulant. You lose credibility. Your point is still lost because the interaction becomes about the interruption itself, not the content.

## The Three Responses That Work

These responses are assertive, not aggressive. They regain control without alienating others. They keep the focus on your contribution.

### 1. The "Hold That Thought" Bridge

This technique acknowledges the interrupter but immediately signals you're not done. It's a quick verbal block that keeps your momentum.

    - **What to say:** "Just a moment," "Let me finish this point," or "One second, I'm almost there."
    - **How to do it:** Speak firmly but not loudly. Maintain eye contact with the interrupter, then pivot back to the rest of the room or your notes. Don't pause for their permission. Just state it and continue.
    - **Scenario:** You're outlining a new strategy. A colleague excitedly interjects with a related idea.
    - **What you say instead:** "That's a great point, [colleague's name]. Let me just finish explaining the current phase, then we can discuss your idea." Then immediately continue your sentence where you left off.
    - **Why it works:** You acknowledge them, so you don't seem rude. But you clearly state you're not finished. You set the boundary and maintain control. It's a professional way to say, "My turn."

### 2. The "Loop Back" Redirect

This is for when an interruption has successfully diverted the conversation. You bring it back to your original thread, showing you haven't forgotten your point.

    - **What to say:** "Circling back to my original point about X..." or "Before we move on, I want to complete my thought on Y."
    - **How to do it:** Wait for a natural break in the new conversation, or create one with a subtle gesture. State your intention to return to your point clearly.
    - **Scenario:** You were discussing budget allocations. An interruption shifted the discussion to project staffing.
    - **What you say instead:** "Thanks, [interrupter's name], for that insight on staffing. Circling back to the budget discussion, my point was that we need to reallocate 15% from marketing to R&D."
    - **Why it works:** You demonstrate persistence and focus. You show you're not easily sidetracked. This also signals to others that your contributions are important enough to revisit.

### 3. The "Direct Reclaim"

This is the most direct. Use it when the interruption is blatant or repeated. It requires confidence and a calm demeanor.

    - **What to say:** "[Interrupter's name], I wasn't finished," or "I'd like to complete my statement."
    - **How to do it:** Make direct eye contact. Use a firm, level tone. Do not raise your voice. Do not get emotional. Just state the fact.
    - **Scenario:** You are presenting a solution, and a peer repeatedly talks over you to offer their own.
    - **What you say instead:** "John, I wasn't finished explaining the implementation details. Let me complete that, then I'd be happy to hear your thoughts."
    - **Why it works:** It's unambiguous. It sets a clear boundary. When delivered calmly, it communicates authority and self-respect without resorting to aggression. It forces the interrupter to acknowledge their action.

## The Sentence That Re-establishes Your Point

Once you've regained the floor, don't just pick up where you left off. Re-anchor your point. This ensures your message lands despite the disruption.

Use this structure:

**"To reiterate my point on [topic], [your main idea/conclusion]."**

For example:

    - "To reiterate my point on the Q3 marketing budget, we need to reallocate 15% to digital ads for immediate impact."
    - "To reiterate my point on client feedback, the main takeaway is that our onboarding process needs simplification."

This sentence immediately brings the conversation back to your core message, ensuring clarity and impact.

## When Gender Changes the Game: Why Women Get Interrupted More

Research consistently shows women are interrupted more often than men in professional settings. This isn't just anecdotal; studies from Yale, George Washington University, and others confirm this pattern. The challenge for women: asserting yourself without being perceived as "aggressive," "emotional," or "difficult" – labels men rarely face for the same behavior.

For women, the "Direct Reclaim" (e.g., "I wasn't finished") can be effective, but it requires an even greater degree of calm and control. The perception penalty is real. Here's how to adapt:

    - **Leverage Allies:** If you have a trusted colleague, agree on a signal. They can say, "Let's hear [Your Name]'s full thought on this before we jump ahead." This shifts the burden of confrontation.
    - **Softer Openings for Direct Reclaim:** Instead of a blunt "I wasn't finished," try: "Thank you for that, but I'd like to complete my thought first." Or, "Just to wrap up my point..." This softens the delivery while maintaining firmness.
    - **Project Confidence, Not Aggression:** Maintain a steady, lower vocal tone. Use open body language. Avoid fidgeting. Your non-verbal cues are crucial in reinforcing your verbal assertion without triggering negative stereotypes.
    - **The "Hold That Thought" Bridge is Gold:** This technique is particularly effective for women because it's assertive without being confrontational. It's a quick, professional pivot that retains control.

The goal is the same: to be heard. But the path to achieving it often requires a more nuanced approach for women.
